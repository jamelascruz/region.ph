Region 9 - Zamboanga Peninsula

Administrative divisions

Zamboanga Peninsula (Tagalog: Tangway ng Zamboanga; Chavacano: Peninsula de Zamboanga; Cebuano: Lawis sa Zamboanga) is an administrative region in the Philippines, designated as Region IX. It consists of three provinces (Zamboanga del Norte, Zamboanga Sibugay and Zamboanga del Sur) including four cities (Dapitan, Dipolog, Pagadian, Isabela) and the highly urbanized Zamboanga City. Prior to the passage of Executive Order No. 36 in 2001, the region was known as Western Mindanao. Although Zamboanga City remains the region's cultural, economic, and educational hub, Pagadian has been recognized as its new regional center.

History of Zamboanga Peninsula

The Zamboanga peninsula was a wide region home to different ethnic groups during the ancient age, the greatest of which being the Subanen people. Subsequently, the region's southern coastal portions came under the influence of the Javanese Majapahit Empire, albeit the empire never conquered the area.

The Sultanate of Sulu dominated the southern regions of the peninsula in the 14th century. Malay missionaries introduced Islam in the southern Philippines during the late 15th and early 16th centuries. Sharif Kabungsuwan, a Johore-born missionary of Malay and Arab heritage, founded the Sultanate of Maguindanao, after which Mindanao is named. The Sultanate of Sulu also occupied the whole island, with the exception of the present-day Caraga area and the eastern sections of the Davao region, ranging from the Zamboanga Peninsula to modern-day Davao City, while the Sultanate of Sulu lost its lands in Zamboanga. Mindanao's sultans led a violent armed battle against the Spanish conquest, particularly under the leadership of Muhammad Kudarat. They quickly formed an alliance with the Sultanate of Sulu. The region's Muslim residents.

Zamboanga was chosen as the location for the Spanish town and fortress on La Caldera in 1569. (now called Barrio Recodo). Zamboanga was one of Mindanao's primary strongholds, aiding colonization operations in the island's south and paving the way for Christian colonies. It also functioned as a military station, defending the island against foreign invaders as well as Moro pirates and their Chinese allies. The province, which is named after and based on Zamboanga City, was formed in part by Peruvian soldiers sent by Sebastián Hurtado de Corcuera.

The Zamboanga Peninsula was crucial in the Spanish-Moro conflict. It was a regular battleground between Spanish soldiers and Moro pirate assaults. While the Spaniards were successful in establishing churches in the region, they suffered greatly at the hands of Moro invaders and were forced to withdraw from the province on several occasions. While the Spaniards scored a military success by attacking the Sultanate of Sulu multiple times, persistent fighting and attacks continued, giving the Moros a psychological triumph.
