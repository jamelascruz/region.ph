Region 4A - Calabarzon

Administrative divisions

Calabarzon (/klbrzn/), previously known as the Southern Tagalog Mainland, is a Filipino administrative area that includes five provinces: Batangas, Cavite, Laguna, Quezon, and Rizal, as well as one highly urbanized city, Lucena. According to the Philippine Statistical Authority, the area is the most populous in the Philippines, with over 16.1 million residents in 2020, and it is also the country's second most densely inhabited after the National Capital Region. The region is located southeast of Metro Manila and is bounded on the west by Manila Bay, on the east by Lamon Bay and the Bicol Region, on the south by Tayabas Bay and the Sibuyan Sea, and on the north by Central Luzon. Mount Makiling is one of its highlights.

History of CALABARZON

The region's name is an abbreviation for its five constituent provinces: Cavite, Laguna, Batangas, Rizal, and Quezon. Calabarzon had composed the historical area known as Southern Tagalog, together with the Mimaropa region, the province of Aurora, and other portions of Metro Manila, until they were divided in 2002 by Executive Order No. 103.

On June 5, 1901, a convention was held to decide whether or not the province of Manila should acquire the province of Morong, which had been determined to be incapable of self-sufficiency. On June 11, Act No. 137 of the First Philippine Commission disbanded Morong and established a new province named after the Philippines' national hero, Jose Rizal, who happened to be a Laguna native. There were 29 municipalities in the new province, 17 from Manila and 12 from Morong. Macario Sakay, a veteran Katipunan member, founded the Tagalog Republic in the Rizal highlands in 1902. Sakay's Tagalog Republic came to an end in 1906, when he and his troops were misled under the pretence of hosting a national assembly aiming at the self-determination of the Filipino people.

On September 7, 1946, the Third Philippine Republic passed Republic Act No. 14, renaming the province of Tayabas Quezon in honor of Manuel Quezon, the second President of the Philippines (now part of Aurora). Aurora, named after Quezon's wife, was established as a sub-province in 1951.

On September 24, 1972, President Ferdinand Marcos issued Presidential Decree No. 1, which restructured the provinces into 11 regions as part of Marcos' Integrated Reorganization Plan. The IRP established Region IV, known as the Southern Tagalog area, which was the Philippines' biggest region. Region IV included Batangas, Cavite, Laguna, Marinduque, Oriental Mindoro, Occidental Mindoro, Quezon, Rizal, Romblon, and Palawan during the time. Aurora formally became a province independent of Quezon in1979, and it was also included in Region IV.

President Gloria Macapagal Arroyo signed Executive Order No. 103 on May 17, 2002, reorganizing the Southern Tagalog area. Region IV was divided into two distinct areas due to its size: Region IV-A (Calabarzon) and Region IV-B. (Mimaropa). Aurora was relocated to Region III, Central Luzon.The next year, Arroyo signed Executive Order No. 246, designating Calamba as the region's regional center.

Demographics

Calabarzon has the greatest population of any region in the Philippines, with 16.1 million people. The population growth rate of 3.07% between 2000 and 2010 declined from 3.91% between 1990 and 2000, a pattern that aligned with the rest of the country. Calabarzon has a life expectancy of 68.9 years for males and 75.2 years for women. Calabarzon is home to an estimated 356,000 Overseas Filipino Workers.

Tagalogs make up the great bulk of Calabarzon residents. Region IV-A is expected to have 5.8 million Tagalogs. Taal, in particular, is regarded the "Heartland of Tagalog Culture" and is now the "hub" of Tagalog culture and people. Calabarzon also has a significant population of individuals of Chinese and Spanish heritage due to Chinese immigration and Spanish colonialism, respectively. Owing to the vast number of Tagalog locals, the majority of people in Calabarzon speak Tagalog. Filipino, a Tagalog dialect, predominates in the region and serves as a lingua franca for Tagalog speakers of other dialects. Calabarzonians also speak English. Chavacano, a creole language, was previously widely spoken in Cavite.

The Catholic Church, which accounts for 80% of the national population, is home to the vast majority of Calabarzon's inhabitants. The Iglesia ni Cristo, the Philippine Independent Church, and the Seventh-day Adventist Church are all present in the region. Muslims are also present in Calabarzon, albeit they are in the minority.
