Region 2 - Cagayan Valley

Administrative divisions

Cagayan Valley is a Filipino administrative area in the northeastern part of the island of Luzon. It is made up of five provinces in the Philippines: Batanes, Cagayan, Isabela, Nueva Vizcaya, and Quirino. Cauayan, Ilagan, Santiago, and Tuguegarao are the four chartered cities in the area.

Cagayan Valley

Archeological findings from the Paleolithic Era show that modern humans' predecessors arrived in Cagayan as early as 500,000 years ago. In quest of game, man may have followed huge beasts into the valley. The Agtas were most likely the first modern people to settle in the wide Cagayan Valley region, followed by different Malayo-Polynesian tribes that created culturally similar but ethically separate societies on the Cagayan plains.

In 1572, Spanish explorer Juan de Salcedo visited the coast of Cagayan and discovered the inhabitants trading with Chinese and Japanese merchants. After driving out Japanese pirates from the Cagayan coast in 1582, the Spanish opted to establish at Lallo, which they dubbed Nueva Segovia. In 1595, Nueva Segovia became the seat of a diocese that included all of northern Luzon.

Cagayan was the name given by the Spaniards to the whole northeastern region of the island of Luzon, as well as several tiny islands in the Balintang Channel. The southern portion of the valley was constituted into a Nueva Vizcaya politico-military region in 1839. Isabela was founded in 1856 by combining sections of Cagayan and Nueva Vizcaya. With the creation of the partido Itawes in 1889 and the comandancia Apayao in1890, Cagayan lost even more land. In 1908, the Americans defined Cagayan's current boundaries. The United States Philippine Commission passed Act No. 209 in 1901, thereby establishing the Provincial Government of Cagayan. Cagayan was designated as a grand division of the Philippine Islands in 1917, as stated in Act No. 2711. The province was then divided into 24 municipalities, with Tuguegarao serving as the capital.

During WWII, Japanese forces landed at Aparri town few days after the bombing of Pearl Harbor in December 1941. In 1945, the valley was once again crucial in Japanese attempts to protect it as a secure route of retreat to Taiwan. Filipino guerillas and Ilocano American troops pushed the Japanese from the Cordilleras.

Cagayan saw the full force of the tobacco monopoly in the late 18th century. Tobacco cultivation, a major item of commerce and consumption, was previously outlawed. In 1787, anti-monopoly revolts erupted, and locals who wished to continue farming tobacco abandoned several communities near the highlands. Fifteen years later, tobacco planting was permitted in the valley, and Cagayan quickly became the archipelago's single greatest source of cash crops. The migration of Ilokano into the valley aided in the growth of agriculture in the region. The large number of Ilokano settlers allowed the Iloko language to supersede Ybanag as the area lingua franca by the mid-nineteenth century.

Demographics

According to the 2020 Census of Population and Housing, the population of Region II - Cagayan Valley as of 01 May 2020 is 3,685,744. (2020 CPH). In 2020, this will account for around 3.38 percent of the Filipino population.

Cagayan is home to three significant ethnic groups: the Ybanags, Itawits, and Malawegs. The dominating group, the Ybanags, are reputed to be the tallest among the ethno-linguistic groups throughout the Philippines.

The Cagayan Valley area, or Region 2, is strategically placed in the northeastern half of mainland Luzon. It is flanked to the east by the Sierra Madre Mountains, to the west by the Cordillera Mountains, and to the south by the Caraballo Mountains.
