Region 13 - Caraga

Administrative divisions

Caraga, formally known as area XIII, is a Philippine administrative area located in the northeastern part of Mindanao. It includes five provinces: Agusan del Norte, Agusan del Sur, Dinagat Islands, Surigao del Norte, and Surigao del Sur, as well as one heavily populated city. Butuan City serves as the regional hub.

History of Caraga

The Rajahnate of Butuan reigned over what is now Agusan del Norte and Butuan during pre-colonial times. It had a significant impact on all of Caraga, parts of Northern Mindanao, and the western edge of Bohol. Butuan was at odds with the Sultanate of Ternate in the present-day Indonesian Moluccas, according to archives. The Ternateans would assault and loot Butuan's wealth, but Butuan would always win. The Rajahnate of Butuan was known to have amicable connections with the Rajahnate of Cebu, which it regarded an ally. Butuan's Rajahnate grew into a strong Hindu monarchy noted for its goldsmithing and boat-building. Butuans utilized massive boats known as balangay to transport large groups of soldiers. The National Museum of the Philippines and other worldwide institutions have found and preserved relics of these gold crafts and huge boats. Butuan was also friendly with the Kingdom of Champa in what is now central Vietnam. Butuans were able to cross the Sulu and South China Seas to reach Champa for commerce. While the Rajahnate of Butuan was still in power, the indigenous lumads of Caraga's interior were free and had developed their own democratic societies. Their communities were renowned for their understanding of nature, medicine, mythology, and warfare. Chinese traders subsequently arrived in Butuan, and Sino-Butuan trade became the focal point of Champa-Butuan relations, with both nations fighting for better trade with Chinese traders. Butuan eventually began to deteriorate as a result of Ternate's enormous attacks. Cebu was unable to assist because it was also being assaulted by Ternate at the time. Similarly, Ternate destroyed the Kedatuan of Dapitan in Bohol, known as "the Venice of the Visayas."

The Magellan expedition met Rajah Siawi and Rajah Kulambo, representatives of the aristocracy of the Surigaonon and Butuanon peoples, respectively, on the island of Limasawa in 1521. He also talks about the "balanghai" (balangay) battleships they sailed in.

The boat-making and goldsmithing traditions were abolished when the Spaniards arrived and subdued the Rajahante of Butuan, who was already weak owing to tensions with the Sultanate of Ternate, and the links between the coastal Butuan and the lumads of the interior became less recognized. The inner lumads were also converted to Christianity, which weakened some of their nature worship customs. The Spanish assaulted the Sultanate of Ternate to prevent any further attacks in Caraga, which they were able to do due to their superior cannon power. With complete control of Caraga, the Spaniards began creating Spanish-style centers in order to maximize their coverage and authority throughout the area, and they were successful. The Spaniards would dominate the nation until the United States beat them in the Spanish-American War, which would result in American control of the whole archipelago.

The "Kalagan," also known as "Caragan" by the Spaniards, inhabited a territory that included the two provinces of Surigao, the northern portion of Davao Oriental, and the eastern Misamis Oriental. Later, the two Agusan provinces were constituted under the administrative jurisdiction of Surigao, and in 1914, they became the autonomous Agusan province. Surigao was separated into Norte and Sur in 1960, and Agusan followed suit in June 1967. While Butuan was only a town of Agusan at the time, the forestry boom in the 1950s lured business to the area. The City Charter of Butuan was enacted on August 2, 1950, by virtue of Republic Act 523.

According to legend, the Caraga Region's first residents were from mainland Asia, followed by Malayans, Arabs, Chinese, Japanese, Spaniards, and Americans. Migrants from the provinces of Visayas and Luzon eventually settled in the region. The majority of its residents speak Cebuano and live in rural regions.

The Caraga Region was established on February 23, 1995, with the passage of Republic Act No. 7901 during the administration of President Fidel Ramos. As part of the newly formed area, the provinces of Agusan del Norte, Agusan del Sur, and Surigao del Norte (of the old Northern Mindanao region) and Surigao del Sur (of the former Southern Mindanao region) were annexed.

Demographics

According to the 2020 census, the region's total population was 2,933,772. During this time, the yearly growth rate exceeded 20%. Agusan del Sur had the most people (700,653) of the five provinces, while Dinagat Islands had the fewest (127,152). Surigao del Norte was the fastest growing province during the last five years, with an annual average growth rate of 1.76%, while Dinagat Islands was the slowest, with a rate of 0.05%.

Butuan and Surigao had populations of 337,063 and 154,137 people, respectively. Butuan saw yearly growth of 1.62% between 2010 and 2015.

Cebuano is commonly spoken in the region, with 33.79% of households speaking it. Surigaonon is spoken by 33.21% of households, but Butuanon (15%), Kamayo (7.06%), and Manobo (4.73%). The remainder speak Boholanon (5.87%), Hiligaynon (2.87%), and various dialects (7.20%). With 74% of the total household population in Caraga, Roman Catholicism is the main religion in the region. Other notable religious minorities include Protestants (20% of total household population) and Aglipayans (6% of total household population).

The bulk of the people in the area are of Visayan descent. The province is home to various minority groups, with a combined population of 675,722 in 1995, accounting for 34.7% of the region's population. The Manobos were the most numerous, accounting for 294,284 people, or 43.55% of the total population of ethnic minorities. Other prominent ethnic groups in the region included the Kamayo, Higa-onon, Banwaon, Umayamnon, Kalagan, and Mamanwa.

The majority of these cultural groups are concentrated in the province of Agusan del Sur.

Region XIII

Caraga, officially the Caraga Administrative Region[3] (or simply known as Caraga Region) and designated as Region XIII, is an administrative region in the Philippines occupying the northeastern section of Mindanao. The region was created through Republic Act No. 7901 on February 23, 1995.[3] The region comprises five provinces: Agusan del Norte, Agusan del Sur, Dinagat Islands, Surigao del Norte, and Surigao del Sur;[4] six cities: Bayugan, Bislig, Butuan, Cabadbaran, Surigao and Tandag; 67 municipalities and 1,311 barangays. Butuan, the most urbanized city in Caraga, serves as the regional administrative center.
