Region 11 - Davao Region

Administrative divisions

Davao area, formally Region XI, is a Philippine administrative area located in the southeastern part of Mindanao. It includes five provinces: Davao de Oro (Compostela Valley), Davao del Norte, Davao del Sur, Davao Occidental, and Davao Oriental, as well as one major city. Davao City serves as the regional hub.

History of Davao Region

The region's history extends back to when several tribes populated the area. The Manobos, Mandayas, Kalagans, Mansakas, and Bagobos are said to have inhabited the area. These are the same tribes who established the little villages and communities that formed Mindanao.

Maguindanao period

The Sultanate of Maguindanao previously included the Davao Region. Maguindanaons, Maranaos, and Tausugs from the Sultanate of Sulu were among the first to settle in the region. Datu Bago was a Datu in Davao Gulf who fought against the Spanish Empire's invasion of the region.

The initial European encounter

The Davao Gulf region was the first location in the country to have contact with Europeans, with such encounters occurring as early as the 16th century. In seeing and visiting the region, the Portuguese were far earlier than the Spaniards, who colonized it much later. Francisco Serrano was shipwrecked in the shallow seas and coral reefs of Cape San Agustn in what is now the province of Davao Oriental in 1512. Strong winds drove Francisco de Castro, a Portuguese ship captain, to the southern shore of Mindanao in 1538. He baptized numerous local chieftains.

Around January 1546, Francis Xavier, a Jesuit priest, departed Malacca for the Molucca Islands, then known as the Spice Islands, where the Portuguese had small colonies, and preached the Gospel to the people of Amboyna, Ternate, Baranura, and other minor islands for a year and a half. several believe that he landed on the island of Mindanao during this mission, which is verified by several seventeenth-century historians and in the Bull of Canonization published in 1623. It is also said that he was the first to preach the Gospel in Mindanao.

Administration in Spain

For generations, the tribes lived in relative peace until the Spanish came in the region in 1847, led by the daring Spanish trader Don Jose Oyanguren. Datu Bago, a Kalagan Moro chieftain, ruled the territory that is now Davao City during the time. Don Oyanguren sought to seize the territory governed by Datu Bago; although he initially failed, the Moro chieftain finally fled his people to reside near Mount Apo. In the year 1848, the town of Davao, then known as Nueva Vergara by the Spaniards, was founded.

Don Oyanguren tried to improve the area. Although the Spanish eventually seized control of the region's ports, the people remained.

Administration of the United States

Spanish authority in the region ended with the Spanish-American War in 1898. The region's communications and transportation systems were later built by Americans who arrived. During this time, the region saw an increase in individual agricultural ownership. Japanese migration in the region began when two Japanese entrepreneurs, Kyosaburo Ohta and Yoshizo Furukawa, were looking for better agricultural lands in the region to construct abaca and coconut plantations. The Port of Davao opened in 1900, becoming the first international port in the Philippines to be developed in the south.

From 1903 until 1914, the area was a district of Mindanao's old Moro Province. After 1914, the province was superseded by the Department of Mindanao and Sulu, an American colonial entity.

Japanese occupation during WWII

The area was one of the first Philippine regions to be subjected to Japanese control during World War II, when the Japanese occupation of the Philippines started in 1942. The Japanese immigrants in Davao acted as a fifth column, welcoming the Japanese invaders during World War II; these Japanese were hated by the Moro Muslims and disliked by the Chinese. The Moros were judged to be "fully capable of dealing with Japanese fifth columnists and invaders alike.The Moros were to fight the Japanese invaders when they landed at Davao on Mindanao.

Administration of the Philippines

The province of Davao
Prior to Philippine independence in 1946, the whole region was governed by a single province named Davao Province, with Davao City as its seat. The province was one of the largest in the Philippines at the time, covering more than 20,000 square kilometers. (7,700 sq mi). It lasted from 1920 to 1967, when it was divided into three provinces: Davao del Norte, Davao del Sur, and Davao Oriental. Following the partition, Davao City was designated as the regional center.

Davao Region and Southern Mindanao
Region XI, then known as Southern Mindanao, originally included six provinces (Compostela Valley, Davao del Norte, Davao del Sur, Davao Oriental, South Cotabato, and Sarangani), as well as the cities of Davao, Digos, Panabo, Tagum, Samal, General Santos, and Koronadal. On February 23, 1995, Surigao del Sur was transferred to the newly formed region Caraga.
Executive Order No. 36 was issued in September 2001, reorganizing the regions of Mindanao. Region XI was renamed Davao Region, while the provinces of South Cotabato and Sarangani, as well as the cities of General Santos and Koronadal, were relocated to Region XII.
After decades of fighting, the Regional Peace and Order Council pronounced the whole territory "insurgency-free" on October 12, 2022.

Demographics

The population was 5,243,536 according to the 2020 Census. This accounted for 19.97% of the total population of Mindanao, or 4.81% of the total population of the Philippines.

With a total population of 5,243,536 in 2020, Davao Region is the most populated region in Mindanao and the seventh most populous in the country. Davao City, its regional capital, is also the largest city in Mindanao, with an area of 2,444 km2, the largest in the country and one of the largest in the world, and has 1,632,991 inhabitants in 2015, making it the country's third most populous city and the most populous city proper in the entire Visayas-Mindanao region. With about 2,274,913 residents in that year, the Davao Metropolitan Area, the region's principal economic and urban development area, is also the most populated on the island and the third most populous in the country.

The majority of the people in the area speak Cebuano. In schools, companies, trade, and industry, Filipino and English are also commonly spoken and used. Locals in the Davao City Metro Area have either changed to Filipino or considerably mixed Filipino terminology and syntax into their Cebuano speech, resulting in a linguistic phenomena. Minority indigenous communities in the region also have their own languages.

Chinese immigration are common in the region, with a sizable concentration centered in Davao City. Davao Chinatown, along with Binondo in Manila, is one of the two distinct Chinatowns in the Philippines. The region also has a sizable Japanese community, the most of whom live in Davao City. Davao was and still is extensively populated by Filipinos prior to World War II.

The bulk of the region's population is Christian, primarily Catholic; however, Muslims, Buddhists, and Shintoists also live there.

Region XI

Davao Region, formerly called Southern Mindanao (Cebuano: Rehiyon sa Davao; Filipino: Rehiyon ng Davao), is an administrative region in the Philippines, designated as Region XI.[4] It is situated at the southeastern portion of Mindanao and comprises five provinces: Davao de Oro, Davao del Norte, Davao del Sur, Davao Oriental and Davao Occidental. The region encloses the Davao Gulf, and its regional center is Davao City. Dávao is the Hispanicized pronunciation of daba-daba, the Bagobo word for "fire".
